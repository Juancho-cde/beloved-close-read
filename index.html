Where We Are in the Story (Ch. 20–24)

This section of Beloved is a pivotal moment in both the storyline and narration. The novel shifts into a sequence of monologues (Sethe, Danver then Beloved, then altogether?) that become increasingly more difficult to distinguish from one another, then Chapter 24 pulls outward into Paul D’s memory of Sweet Home, reconnecting with personal hauntings.

Quick Orientation

  • Ch. 20–23: A series of first person narration monologues. The narration becomes intimate, fragmented, and resembles characters' feelings and thoughts.
  • Ch. 20: Sethe argues her act as protection, she feels like she did the right thing and Beloved understands that.
  • Ch. 21: Denver’s perspective highlights her fear of Sethe, loving Beloved and her father, and the tension between “inside” safety and “outside” danger.
  • Ch. 22: Beloved’s voice seems to expand beyond one person, which seemingly hints at her representing something more than just Sethe’s daughter, maybe a collective history or some sort of memory.
  • Ch. 23: The voices converge into repetition, blurring the perspective and highlighting interconnectedness. Hard to follow and meant to be that way.
  • Ch. 24: Paul D’s chapter takes us back outside of 124 for the first time, narrates his memories of Sweet Home, Sixo's death and Halle

Reading Focus for Today

  • Track how voice and structure change across the sequence (who speaks, how clearly, and with what freedom do they seek?).
  • Notice what “freedom” looks like for each individual, especially when the past keeps governing the present.
  • How do we see this theme of “In-betweeness”, how could this create more complex relationships between characters in the novel?

Chapter 20

“If I hadn't killed her she would have died and that is something I could not bear to happen to her.”

Language & Form

  • Frames Sethe’s choice as made out of necessity.
  • Diction like “bear” ties in Sethe's emotions, impossible for her not to do it.
  • Raises the moral dilemma once again, is this justified?

Meaning

  • Sethe is “free,” yet still governed by the fear and threat of slavery.
  • Love becomes a questionable justification tool.
  • Juxtaposition of wanting what is best for beloved

Chapter 21

“I always knew he was coming. Something was holding him up. The river flooded; the boat sank and he had to make a new one...He was coming and it was a secret.”

Language & Form

  • Seems to justify and yearn for an escape from 124.
  • Secrecy is highlighted in the diction, this is information only for Denver
  • The list of obstacles gives the reader a sense of hopelessness, and seems unlikely he will ever come .

Meaning

  • Draws some parallels to Sethe escaping Sweet Home.
  • Seems to be in a state of in between, two realities outside and inside of 124
  • The product of freedom seems like she is not fully free either.

Chapter 22

“We are not crouching now we are standing but my legs are like my dead man's eyes I cannot fall because there is no room to.”

Language & Form

  • Collective pronoun “We” expands Beloved beyond one person.
  • Short syntax mirrors physical restriction.
  • Death imagery is mixed in with the living/blurring the boundaries.

Meaning

  • Beloved occupies the in-between: dead ↔ alive singular ↔ collective.
  • The haunting becomes historical, not just a private family problem.
  • Beloved seems to be different, in between character and instigator.

Chapter 23

“You are mine.
You are mine.
You are mine.”

Language & Form

  • Repetition functions like incantation
  • Extremely short syntax
  • Voice is very hard to locate.

Meaning

  • The interconnectedness of the characters seem to all be united by something.
  • Questions surrounding love and possession, does something need to be yours so you can love it?

Chapter 24

“Nobody counted on Garner dying. Nobody thought he could...Now ain't that slavery or what is it?”

Language & Form

  • The rhetorical question makes a strong suggestion.
  • Repetition of Nobody creates the illusion of security as coming from a collective

Meaning

  • Ch. 24 re-anchors the story using narration.
  • Again asking whether people can ever truly be free from slavery.
  • “In between” past and future traumas

Conclusion

Chapters 20-24 demonstrates that “Beloved” is a novel about in-between-ness. Freedom, love and morality is not as clean cut as one might expect and Morrison reveals this through her characters.

Key Takeaways

  • Fragmentation of text is intentional → Contributes to a deeper understanding of the work
  • There is tension in the removal of borders, physical and mental

Discussion Questions

  • Does (re)memory fuel the characters or tie them to the past?
  • When does love become ownership in these chapters, are these two things dependent on each other?
  • What overall assumptions can we make about Beloved and her relationship with the other characters?
